### 1. Qualys (QLYS) — $5.6B · composite 66

**What they do.** Qualys is one of the original cloud-based vulnerability management (VM) vendors: agents and scanners inventory an enterprise's IT assets, find unpatched software and misconfigurations, and prioritize fixes. The platform has expanded into compliance, web-app scanning, and its newer Enterprise TruRisk Management (ETM) suite, which rolls all exposure data into one risk view.

**Business model.** Pure subscription SaaS, sold per-asset/per-module, mostly annual prepaid — which produces enviable economics: **83% GAAP gross margin, 47% adjusted-EBITDA margin, free-cash-flow margin guided in the low 40s**. Q1 2026: revenue $175.6M (+10% y/y), EPS up 16.8%; FY26 guidance raised to $721–727M (+8–9%).

**Moat.** High switching costs (VM data and compliance workflows embed deeply into security operations and audit processes), a 25-year brand in the space, and the structural advantage that its agent footprint is already deployed across customers' fleets. It is, however, a *feature-scale* moat, not a platform-scale one.

**Growth trajectory.** Decelerating: high-single-digit growth versus 20%+ five years ago. This is now a mature, cash-gushing security asset, not a growth story.

**Cons / risks.** The VM category is commoditizing and converging with the platforms — CrowdStrike, Wiz, Palo Alto, and direct rival Tenable all sell exposure management now, and channel-led megavendors can bundle it at a discount. Qualys is a single-product-line company; if ETM fails to reignite growth, the terminal-value question gets loud. At ~7.7x forward sales it isn't expensive for the margin profile, but there's no multiple expansion story without reacceleration.

**Fair take.** A profitable, defensible cash machine priced like one. You're buying durable FCF and the option on ETM — not growth. The screen likes it for exactly that: solid fundamentals (66) plus a strong tape (technical 73).

### 2. Dropbox (DBX) — $7.1B · composite 66

**What they do.** File sync, sharing, and storage for consumers and SMBs (700M+ registered users), plus e-signature (HelloSign/Dropbox Sign), document workflow (DocSend), and its AI bet: **Dash**, a universal AI search across a company's scattered SaaS content.

**Business model.** Freemium subscription; the paid conversion funnel is the whole engine. Growth is essentially **flat** (FY26 guide $2.497–2.512B), and management has pivoted the equity story to capital return: Q1 alone saw **$370M of buybacks (14.3M shares)**, a new **$900M buyback authorization**, and unlevered FCF guidance of **≥$1.055B** — a ~15% uFCF yield on the market cap.

**Moat.** Modest. Brand and cross-platform neutrality, an enormous installed base, and sync reliability that's still best-in-class — but it competes with storage bundled free into Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, and iCloud. The moat is inertia more than lock-in.

**Growth trajectory.** Revenue roughly flat; paying users ticked *up* unexpectedly in Q1, and early Dash engagement is the first genuinely new demand signal in years — but it's unproven as a revenue line.

**Cons / risks.** This is a **levered equity shrink**: $2.68B of term loans funded buybacks, and stockholders' equity is now *negative* (-$2.0B) — an accounting artifact of repurchases, but real leverage all the same. Net income fell in Q1 on interest expense. If FCF ever falters, the balance sheet flips from tailwind to problem. Secular relevance vs. bundled suites remains the long-run bear case; Dash competes with Microsoft Copilot and Glean.

**Fair take.** A deep-value cannibal: shrinking share count, huge FCF, cheap multiple — with leverage and a no-growth core. Works as a return-of-capital position, not a compounder. The screen's quality signal (84) is doing exactly what it should here: rewarding cash conversion and share shrink.

### 3. Tenable (TENB) — $4.3B · composite 66

**What they do.** Tenable owns **Nessus** — the most widely deployed vulnerability scanner in the world and near-universal muscle memory for security practitioners — and has built it into **Tenable One**, an exposure-management platform spanning cloud, OT/ICS, identity, and attack-surface management.

**Business model.** Subscription (93% of revenue; 96% recurring). Q1 2026: revenue $262.1M (+9.6%), non-GAAP operating margin 23.6% (+320bps y/y), RPO +14.8% to $1.01B. FY26 guidance raised to $1.068–1.078B (+7.4%) with non-GAAP EPS +22% at midpoint. Platform sales momentum is real: Tenable One was 41% of new business (+8pts y/y), 406 new enterprise platform customers, 43 new six-figure accounts.

**Moat.** The Nessus install base is a genuine distribution moat — it's the default in certifications, government STIGs, and pen-test toolkits, giving Tenable bottom-up pull competitors must pay for. Platform consolidation (fewer vendors, more modules) raises switching costs as customers adopt Tenable One.

**Growth trajectory.** Single-digit and decelerated, but *margin expansion is doing the work*: EPS growing ~3x faster than revenue. The bet is that exposure management becomes the mandatory control layer in an AI-accelerated threat landscape.

**Cons / risks.** GAAP operating margin is only 3.3% — the non-GAAP/GAAP gap is stock comp, a real cost. The same platform convergence that threatens Qualys threatens Tenable, from better-funded rivals (Wiz under Google, Palo Alto, CrowdStrike). Federal exposure (a meaningful vertical for Nessus) adds budget-cycle risk. At ~4x forward sales the multiple already prices modest growth.

**Fair take.** The cheapest way into exposure management with a real distribution advantage; you accept single-digit growth and SBC dilution in exchange. Screen drivers: the strongest technical score in the top 5 (82) plus quality 79 — the tape and the cash quality agree.

### 4. Criteo (CRTO) — $1.1B · composite 64

**What they do.** French ad-tech firm, historically the leader in performance retargeting (those follow-you-around product ads), now pivoting into **retail media** — operating the sponsored-ads infrastructure *inside* retailers' sites (think the ad system behind a grocer's search results), monetizing retailers' first-party commerce data.

**Business model.** Takes a cut of media spend; the metric that matters is **Contribution ex-TAC** (revenue minus traffic costs). Q1 2026: revenue $425M; ex-TAC $250M, **down 9%** at constant currency — though excluding a $27M headwind from two retail-media client scope reductions it was +1%, and underlying retail-media contribution grew **+24%** with media spend crossing $1B for the first time. Retention ~90%. FY26 guide: ex-TAC down low-single-digits.

**Moat.** A uniquely deep cross-retailer commerce dataset and direct integrations with hundreds of retailers — real assets in a post-cookie world. But the moat leaks: its largest clients are exactly the retailers big enough to eventually in-house (the "largest retailer" scope loss shows it), and Amazon dominates the category's economics.

**Growth trajectory.** A tale of two businesses: legacy performance media slowly declining (-2%), retail media growing 20%+ underneath client-scope noise. Whether the crossover happens before the legacy decline erodes the P&L is *the* question.

**Cons / risks.** Shrinking top line in guidance, concentration risk just demonstrated, signal loss/privacy regime perpetually hostile, and ad-tech trades at permanently compressed multiples — cheapness alone hasn't rerated this stock for years. The quality score (88, best-in-class buybacks and cash conversion) is why the screen surfaced it, but this is a turnaround, not a steady compounder.

**Fair take.** Genuinely cheap with a genuinely growing segment inside a genuinely declining shell. Suits investors comfortable underwriting a messy transition; wrong for anyone who needs the headline numbers to look good soon.

### 5. JFrog (FROG) — $11.1B · composite 64

**What they do.** JFrog Artifactory is the de-facto **system of record for software binaries** — every build, package, container, and increasingly every ML model an enterprise produces flows through it. Xray adds software-supply-chain security; the platform now stretches into MLOps/AI model management.

**Business model.** Subscription with self-hosted and cloud tiers; cloud just crossed **51% of revenue, growing 50% y/y**. Q1 2026: revenue $154M (**+26%**), net-dollar retention 120%, $1M+ ARR customers +48% to 80, non-GAAP operating margin 21.4%. FY26 guide $628–632M.

**Moat.** Strong and structural: artifact repositories are brutal to migrate (every CI/CD pipeline, build script, and dependency reference points at them), making Artifactory one of the stickiest tools in the developer stack. The AI angle is real — model registries look exactly like artifact registries, and JFrog is the incumbent pattern.

**Growth trajectory.** The only *accelerating* name in this top 5: 26% total growth with the cloud mix-shift still early, NDR 120%, and large-enterprise cohort growth (+48% $1M accounts) suggesting durable expansion.

**Cons / risks.** Priced for it: ~17.6x forward sales, by far the richest in this list — a multiple that assumes years of 20%+ growth, and that gets hit hardest in any risk-off tape. GAAP-unprofitable with heavy stock comp; the screen's quality score (22, worst of the five) is flagging exactly that dilution. Competitive pressure is real from GitHub/GitLab package registries and cloud-native offerings bundling "good enough" artifact storage. DevOps budgets are cyclical with developer headcount.

**Fair take.** The highest-quality growth asset in the screen and the most expensive; the moat justifies a premium, the current premium leaves no room for stumbles. Position sizing matters more here than anywhere else in this list.
